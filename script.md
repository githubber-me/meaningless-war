# The Line

**Runtime:** 3:00 (5400 frames @ 30fps)
**Format:** 1920x1080, 16:9
**Style:** Black stick figures on textured off-white paper. Red is used only for the border, propaganda, and destruction. Red is never generated; it is composited in the edit. No graphic violence.
**Narration:** 181 words, eight blocks, recorded separately (ElevenLabs, one file per block).

---

### 0:00–0:20 — Before

**Visuals:** Blank page. A stick figure appears, followed by a partner, two children and a small house. Across the page, another nearly identical family appears. Quick images of cooking, working, playing, arguing and sleeping.

**Narrator (VO-01):**

> Before the war, there were no heroes. No enemies.
>
> Just people who woke too early, worked too long, and tried to get home before dinner.
>
> On both sides, life was ordinary.
> And that was enough.

---

### 0:20–0:40 — The line

**Visuals:** A large hand enters with a red pencil and draws a vertical line between the families. It writes "US" on one side and "THEM" on the other. Flags rise above both houses.

**Narrator (VO-02):**

> Then someone drew a line.
>
> No mountain stood there. No river.
>
> Just a line, and a story about the people beyond it.

---

### 0:40–1:02 — The story spreads

**Visuals:** Radios, newspapers, phones and podiums appear. Leaders on both sides point across the line.

Rapid propaganda cards alternate:

- THEY WANT WHAT IS OURS
- THEY CANNOT BE TRUSTED
- WE MUST STRIKE FIRST
- WE HAVE NO CHOICE

Reveal that both sides are receiving the same messages. The sequence ends on a single full-screen card: **ENEMY**. The narrator never says the word. The card says it.

**Narrator (VO-03):**

> The story was repeated until fear felt like knowledge.
>
> Questions became disloyalty.
>
> And the people across the line stopped being people.
> They became one word.

---

### 1:02–1:28 — The machinery

**Visuals:** Coins fall into a machine and emerge as bullets, helmets and tanks. Teachers replace faces on a blackboard with flags. Young stick figures receive uniforms. Families wave as trains carry soldiers away.

**Narrator (VO-04):**

> Fear became a speech.
> The speech became an order.
> The order became a uniform.
>
> And frightened people were sent to kill other frightened people.

---

### 1:28–2:00 — War

**Visuals:** Editing accelerates. Rapid stills, 2 to 4 images per second:

- Boots marching from both directions
- Two soldiers with identical frightened expressions
- Artillery flashes
- A child hiding beneath a table
- A bridge collapsing
- A hospital corridor
- A burning schoolbook
- A soldier clutching a family photograph
- The same photograph held by a soldier on the opposite side
- Empty chairs appearing around dinner tables
- Medals pinned to empty uniforms
- Rows of simple crosses growing across the page

**Narrator (VO-05):**

> The line swallowed names, and returned numbers.
>
> Each death justified the next.
>
> Soon, no one remembered how it began.
> Only that too many had died to stop.

**Audio:** Narration ends by 1:52. Music and distant wind continue alone for the final eight seconds. No cuts slower than one per second until 1:55, then hold one frame (the rows of crosses) in silence into the next section.

---

### 2:00–2:22 — Victory

**Visuals:** Two leaders sign a document and shake hands. Confetti falls (black and white; the red rule holds even here). Newspapers on both sides print "VICTORY." Exhausted soldiers return home. Some families embrace; others wait beside empty chairs.

**Narrator (VO-06):**

> Eventually, the leaders met.
>
> They used words that had been available from the beginning.
>
> Both sides announced victory.
>
> The dead did not object.

---

### 2:22–2:42 — The cost

**Visuals:** Pull back to reveal the map. The red border has moved only slightly. The ruins fade into scaffolding as survivors rebuild the same houses, bridge and school.

**Narrator (VO-07):**

> The line moved a few centimetres.
>
> Parents buried children.
>
> Children inherited medals, debts, and stories about people they had never met.

---

### 2:42–3:00 — Ending

**Visuals:** A child approaches the border from each side. They look at one another. One produces an eraser. Together, they erase the red line. The two flags fall away. The children begin drawing a tree where the border stood.

As they draw, a new hand holding a red pencil enters frame from above. It hesitates over the page.

Hard cut to white before the pencil touches down.

**Narrator (VO-08):**

> If peace was always how it would end...
>
> why could it not have been how it began?

**Final card:**

> **NO LINE IS WORTH A GENERATION.**

Hold for three seconds. Fade to white.

## Rules that do not bend

1. Red appears only as: the border line, propaganda card accents, destruction glow, the final pencil. Nothing else is ever red.
2. All generated imagery is pure black and white. Red is composited in Remotion or the edit, never generated.
3. The narrator never says the word "enemy." Only the card does.
4. The hesitating hand never touches the page. The cut to white comes first.
5. The final card holds a full three seconds before fade.
